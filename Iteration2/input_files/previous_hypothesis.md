The poor explanatory power ($R^2 \approx 0.001$) of isotropic interaction models and the observed anisotropy in approach velocities suggest that vortex interactions are not merely functions of distance $r$, but are governed by the **relative orientation of the vorticity vectors ($\hat{\omega}_i \cdot \hat{\omega}_j$) and the alignment of the inter-vortex vector ($\hat{r}_{ij}$)**. 

I hypothesize that the effective interaction potential $V(r, \theta, \phi)$ can be decomposed into a **Biot-Savart-like dipolar component** that accounts for the observed helicity-dependent attraction. Specifically, I propose that the interaction force is dominated by the projection of the vorticity vectors onto the plane perpendicular to the separation vector. By replacing the isotropic potential with a **directional potential model $V(r, \Theta) = f(r) \cdot \cos^n(\Theta)$**, where $\Theta$ is the angle between the vorticity vectors, we can capture the "attraction-by-alignment" mechanism. This approach will replace the failed isotropic power-law fit with a multi-variate regression that accounts for the vortex-vortex alignment, effectively filtering out the stochastic noise caused by the ambient turbulent background. This will allow us to quantify the "effective charge" of a vortex as a function of its local helicity density, providing a predictive model for vortex merger events that is invariant to the tracking artifacts identified in previous iterations.
