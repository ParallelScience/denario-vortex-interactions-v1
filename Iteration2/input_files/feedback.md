The current analysis has reached a point of diminishing returns regarding "effective theory" parameters. The failure to detect mergers and the weak drift signal suggest that the current framework—treating vortices as point-like particles in a Langevin potential—is fundamentally mismatched with the physics of 3D turbulence.

**Critical Weaknesses:**
1. **The "Point-Vortex" Fallacy:** You are attempting to fit a pairwise potential $V(r)$ to centroid drift. In 3D turbulence, vortex dynamics are dominated by non-local induction (Biot-Savart) and intense local stretching/tilting, not a central force potential. The "power-law" fit is likely an artifact of the background flow field rather than an interaction potential.
2. **Merger/Dissipation Disconnect:** You report ~0 mergers and only 6 dissipation events. This indicates that your tracking/thresholding is likely too restrictive or that the "vortex" definition is capturing long-lived, stable structures while missing the transient, high-energy interactions that actually drive the cascade.
3. **Langevin Drift Bias:** Subtracting a low-pass filtered velocity field to isolate "interaction" is physically suspect. The background flow *is* the interaction in 3D turbulence. By masking the cores, you are removing the very structures that define the local velocity field.

**Actionable Recommendations:**
1. **Pivot from Potential to Topology:** Stop trying to fit $V(r)$. Instead, focus on the **Vortex Stretching Term** ($\vec{\omega} \cdot \nabla \vec{u}$). Calculate the correlation between the local strain tensor $\mathbf{S}$ and the vorticity vector $\vec{\omega}$ along the trajectories. This is the actual "effective theory" of 3D turbulence.
2. **Re-evaluate the "Vortex" Definition:** The Q-criterion is sensitive to the threshold. If you have 0 mergers, your threshold is likely too high, effectively isolating "islands" of vorticity that never interact. Perform a sensitivity analysis: lower the Q-threshold to capture more transient structures and observe if the merger rate increases.
3. **Abandon the Langevin Model:** The Langevin approach assumes the system is a gas of particles. It is not. It is a fluid. Replace the Langevin drift analysis with a **Conditional Averaging** approach: compute the average velocity gradient tensor $\langle \nabla \vec{u} \rangle$ conditioned on the distance $r$ to the nearest vortex core. This will reveal the structural influence of a vortex on its surroundings without assuming a central force.
4. **Focus on the "Shredding" Metric:** You found a correlation between $\alpha_{max}$ and $\tau_{norm}$. This is your most valuable result. Instead of N-body simulations, perform a **Lagrangian budget analysis**: track the evolution of $\Gamma$ and $\omega_{mag}$ for individual vortices and correlate these changes with the local Okubo-Weiss parameter $Q$ and the strain-to-rotation ratio $\alpha$. This directly addresses the "stability" goal.
5. **Simplify:** The directional potential $V(r, \Theta)$ is noise-dominated. Drop it. Focus on the relationship between the vortex-core helicity $H$ and its lifetime. Does high helicity correlate with longer survival? This is a much more robust physical question than fitting power-law exponents to weak drift signals.

**Summary for next iteration:** Stop treating vortices as particles in a potential field. Treat them as dynamic, evolving structures in a strain-dominated flow. The "effective theory" should describe the *stability* of the core under strain, not the *force* between centroids.
