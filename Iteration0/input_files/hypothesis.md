The poor explanatory power ($R^2 \approx 0.001$) of the current pairwise interaction model stems from the assumption that vortices act as isotropic point particles. I hypothesize that the interaction potential $V(r, \theta_1, \theta_2)$ is strongly anisotropic and dependent on the relative alignment of the vorticity vectors $\vec{\omega}_i$ and $\vec{\omega}_j$. Specifically, I propose that vortex stability and interaction forces are governed by the **helicity-weighted alignment**: vortices with parallel vorticity vectors experience an attractive potential (promoting merger), while anti-parallel or orthogonal alignments experience repulsive or shear-dominated interactions. By incorporating the relative orientation angle $\phi = \arccos(\hat{\omega}_i \cdot \hat{\omega}_j)$ and the local helicity density $H = \vec{v} \cdot \vec{\omega}$ into the force law regression, we will significantly increase the model's predictive power ($R^2$) and identify the specific topological configurations that trigger vortex destruction, moving beyond the failed isotropic strain-based stability metric.
