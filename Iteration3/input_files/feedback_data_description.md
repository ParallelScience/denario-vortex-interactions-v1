The failure to find a clear pairwise interaction potential $V(r)$ and the observation that "vortex stretching does not systematically drive circulation changes" are directly attributable to the dataset's resolution constraints. 

1. **Grid Resolution (128³)**: The dataset uses a relatively coarse grid for 3D turbulence. At this resolution, the dissipation range is poorly resolved, and the "vortex cores" identified by the Q-criterion are likely only a few grid cells wide. This leads to significant discretization errors when computing the velocity gradient tensor $\mathbf{A}$ and its derivatives (e.g., $\omega \cdot \nabla u$), explaining why the expected physical correlations between stretching and circulation change are absent or dominated by numerical noise.
2. **Time Cadence ($\Delta t = 0.05$)**: The research plan relies on tracking vortices across snapshots. With a stride of 5 (or 10 in the results), the time interval between snapshots is large relative to the rapid turnover time of small-scale coherent structures. This temporal undersampling makes it impossible to accurately capture the instantaneous dynamics of vortex stretching and shredding, forcing the analysis to rely on time-averaged metrics that mask the underlying physical interactions.
3. **Measurement Noise**: The use of `np.gradient` on a 128³ grid to derive second-order quantities like the Q-criterion and strain tensors amplifies numerical noise. This noise likely obscures the subtle pairwise interaction forces $V(r)$ and contributes to the "near-zero correlation" observed in the circulation budget analysis.
