The observed ambiguity in the Lévy index (varying from 1.24 to 1.80 depending on the `max_matching_distance`) is directly attributable to the dataset's **time cadence** and the **greedy nearest-neighbor tracking** approach. 

The dataset provides snapshots at $\Delta t = 0.05$ intervals. In turbulent flows, vortex centers can undergo rapid displacement or topological changes (mergers/splits) within this interval. Because the dataset description specifies a simple greedy nearest-neighbor matching algorithm, the analysis is constrained by the inability to distinguish between physical vortex motion and tracking errors (e.g., "jumps" caused by the disappearance of one vortex and the birth of another nearby). The sensitivity of the Lévy index to the `max_matching_distance` confirms that the "heavy tails" are partially an artifact of the tracker's failure to handle the discrete time sampling of a high-velocity, high-vorticity field, rather than purely intrinsic vortex dynamics. Consequently, conclusions regarding the statistical nature of vortex motion (Lévy flight vs. Gaussian walk) are affected by this tracking-threshold dependency.
